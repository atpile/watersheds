This map shows how surface water flows in the United States.

When you tap on the map, the program highlights everything that is upstream from the point where you tap in red, and all the downstream area in blue. You can also pan around or zoom in or out on the map as usual.

The URL tracks the current map view and coloring, so you can copy/paste the URL if you want to share a specific view with someone.

The coloring is based on the level-12 watershed regions as published by the US Geological Survey. There are just over 100,000 of these regions covering the United States; each one is a polygon defining a local drainage basin that is five or ten miles or so across. The data in this program came from the Watershed Boundary Dataset, which contains the geometry of the watershed regions and the flow relationships between them.

The region that is immediately under the location where you tap is drawn in yellow; you might have to zoom in pretty far on the map to see it. The red area on the map shows all the regions that are upstream of the yellow one, and the blue area shows all the retions that are downstream.

Some interesting things to note:

  • Most locations do not have much upstream (red) area. The only locations with large upstream areas tend to be in or very near rivers.
  • Click on the Mississippi River near downtown New Orleans to see a huge example.
  • Notice how the drainage direction jumps from one side to the other as you move back and forth across the continental divides in both the eastern and western mountains.
  • Notice that there is a large area covering most of Nevada and the western part of Utah where the downstream drainage never makes it to an ocean. This area is called the Great Basin.
